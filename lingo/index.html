The Lingo Library

Introduction

This is a collection of puzzles from my favorite parts of Lingo.

While Lingo is a great game, it takes about 20 hours to get to the part of the game that this page simulates. See Opinions for a big ol' rant.

So I'd rather just show you the good stuff. Each puzzle has annotations to tell you what exactly a given clue means, and I've even added some extra hints and clues in case those become necessary.

Opinions about Lingo

Lingo is a language-based puzzle game about guessing words based on clues you're given in a strange system governed by the colors and heights of boxes. I liked it. I did not like the 10+ hours of wandering around looking for where a specific puzzle was. The design of the world seems to actively avoid letting you know where you should be looking next.

BUT, eventually there are a few areas with puzzles that I really liked that were impossible to properly express in any way other than the bizarre system the game builds for describing the clues to the words it wants.

How Lingo Works

Puzzles in Lingo are shown on panels on the sides of cubes. The height and color of the cube tells you some quality about the solution to the puzzle. Every puzzle has only one solution. Sometimes, puzzles combine together clues given by multiple cubes, and that's my favorite stuff in the game.

Height

Clues that are HIGH have to do with the SOUNDS of the word. For example, a clue might ask you to find a word that sounds the same as another word, or contains the same sounds but rearranged, or to write the sound that something makes, etc.

Clues that are at a MIDDLE height have to do with the LETTERS that make up the word. For example, a clue might ask you to find an anagram of a word, or a word contained in another word.

Clues that are LOW have to do with the DEFINITION of the word. These clues are the most varied. They ask you to take the rule given by the HIGH and MIDDLE meanings of whatever color the cube has and apply it to the literal concept given by the clue.

White - Unchanging

White clues ask you not to change the given word.

A high white clue means the solution sounds the same as the given word.
Example: Way ➔ Weigh, Right ➔ Write, Depend ➔ Deepened.

A middle white clue means the solution is spelled the same as the given word. Those ones aren't very hard to solve.
Example: Antidisestablishmentarianism ➔ Antidisestablishmentarianism

A low white clue means the solution has (roughly) the same meaning as the given word.
Example: Reveal ➔ Uncover, Yell ➔ Holler

Black - Reversal, Opposite, Complement

Black clues ask you to somehow reverse the given word.

A high black clue means the solution sounds the same as the given word, but backwards.
Example: Cat ➔ Tack

A middle white clue means the solution is spelled the same as the given word. Those ones aren't very hard to solve.
Example: Antidisestablishmentarianism ➔ Antidisestablishmentarianism

A low white clue means the solution has (roughly) the same meaning as the given word.
Example: Reveal ➔ Uncover, Yell ➔ Holler

Red - Reduction

Red clues ask you to remove something from the given word. Notably, they don't change the order of whatever's left.

A high red clue means that the sounds of the solution are present in the word, but there's some extra sounds there that need to be removed.
Example: Tolerant ➔ Taunt. Yep, we're fully caught-cot merger here.

A middle red clue means that the letters of the solution are present in the word, but there's some extra letters that need to be removed.
Example: Hippopotamus ➔ Pot

A low red clue means that the solution is something that is contained within the given word, or something that the given word "has."
Example: Flower ➔ Petal, Door ➔ Doorknob

Blue - Addition

Blue clues ask you to take what's given to you and add to it somehow.

A high blue clue means that the sounds of the solution include the sounds of the clue, but there's some extra sounds that need to be added. (The sounds might be a bit approximate.)
Example: Sun ➔ Resonant, Lore ➔ Lawnmower

A middle blue clue means that each letter of the clue appears in the solution, in that order, but there's some extra letters that need to be added.
Example: Pony ➔ Phony

A low blue clue means that the clue is literally contained within the solution, or the solution "has" the clue as a part of it somehow.
Example: City ➔ County, Tooth ➔ Mouth

Purple - Replacement

Purple clues ask you to replace part of what's given to you. It's like Red and Blue at the same time. Cute, right?

It is intentionally very unclear exactly how much the solution may be different from the clue.

A high purple clue means that while some of the sounds of the correct word are present, you'll need to replace some of the other sounds.
Example: Depend ➔ Descend, Trouble ➔ Bubble

A middle purple clue means that while some of the letters of the correct word are present, you'll need to replace some of the other letters.
Example: Trouble ➔ Troubador

A low purple clue means that the definition of the clue has to be somehow modified to get to the answer.
Example: Cucumber ➔ Pickle

Yellow - Rearranging

Yellow clues ask you to change the placement of the parts of the clue.

A high yellow clue means that the sounds of the clue need to be shuffled in some way to get the solution.
Example: Fine ➔ Knife

A middle yellow clue means that the solution is an anagram of the clue.
Example: State ➔ Taste

A low yellow clue means that the solution is what you get when you shuffle together the clue.
Example: Fruits ➔ Smoothie

Brown - Evolution

Brown clues ask you to write what chronologically follows the given clue.

A middle brown clue means that the solution is the way that the clue would be spelled later.
Example: Hail ➔ Hi, God Be With You ➔ Goodbye

A low brown clue means that the answer happens after the clue happens.
Example: Rain ➔ Rainbow

Brown & Black - The Opposite of That

There's no good color that's the opposite of brown!

One of these weird little checkerboard clues means that you need to say what comes before the clue instead.
Example: Present ➔ Past

Magenta - Specification

Magenta clues ask you to find a more specific example of the given clue.

A high magenta clue asks you to write the sound made by the clue.
Example: Crow ➔ Caw, Lion ➔ Roar

A middle magenta clue means that the solution is an example of the given kind of word. Usually this is like, a part of speech.
Example: Conjunction ➔ But

A low magenta clue means that the solution is an example of the given kind of thing.
Example: Color ➔ Chartreuse

Lime - Generalization

Lime clues ask you to take the clue and say what kind of thing it is. It's the opposite of Magenta, but we ran out of colors that have good opposites.

A high lime clue asks you to write what makes that noise.
Example: Tick ➔ Clock

A middle lime clue means that the solution is the kind of word shown in the clue.
Example: The ➔ Article

A low lime clue means that the solution is the kind of thing in the clue.
Example: Kumquat ➔ Fruit

Lavender - Surroundings

Lavender clues ask you to say what surrounds the clue.

A low lavender clue means that the solution surrounds the clue somehow.
Example: Room ➔ Wall, Picture ➔ Frame, Bird ➔ Nest

Miscellaneous

Gray - Gray means something context-specific, for when the puzzle designer doesn't feel like their idea warrants a whole new color.

Green - Green blocks ask you to find something in the environment, which isn't going to matter much here in the Simulation Realm.

Orange - Orange blocks transform the clue somehow, usually just putting the text through a cipher.

Mint - Mint requires you to remember something from previous puzzles. You probably haven't played those puzzles if you're here. They might be a bit hard for you to solve.
