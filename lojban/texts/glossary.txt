Keywords
When written on its own line, only the word is written, and the subject is omitted. It doesn't say "lo ti vi'ordai cu vofli" to say "Flying". It's just "vofli". The purpose of these keywords is to save space.
When written as a declaration/check that something has or gains that keyword, it's written as the property of that keyword. So to say "Your creatures gain flying" you would say "loi danlu po do lebna lo ka vofli" meaning "Your creatures gain the property of flying"

Targets are written in a way that supposes the targeting at the beginning of the ability, closer to how it actually works per the rules of magic. Example:
  - "Destroy target creature" would be written as, in lojban, "Choose target creature, which we will call thing-1. Destroy thing-1."
  - The word for "choose" for targets in this way is "fargau."
  - Doing it this way is very elegant in lojban and the phrasing is consistent across many examples.
"any target" refers only to things that can be damaged, so a target in this way is called a ka'erselxai.

Replacement effects are written as:
selbasti ro nu [event being replaced] kei lo nu [event it is replaced with] kei
Except when they say "as [event happens] do [an extra thing]", in which case:
ca [event being replaced] [extra thing added to the event]

Triggered abilities are written as:
ba ro nu [trigger event] kei lo nu [action] kei

Intervening if clauses:
IFLE

Reasoning for just using the words for types/subtypes instead of writing them properly with "lo danlu" or "lo rutmi":
  - In english, we don't write "A creature - A human and druid", we just say "Creature - Human Druid"
  - Benefit: The text is shorter, and the types are identifiable by being single words
